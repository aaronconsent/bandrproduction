4140 and 4340 Alloy Steel Machining Guide — Pre-hardened and Annealed

4140 and 4340 are the workhorses of oilfield-grade alloy steel — used for wellhead spools, downhole tool bodies, shafts, and structural components. Machine easily in both annealed and pre-hardened (~28–32 HRC) conditions with standard shop tooling. This guide covers feeds, speeds, and the small differences that matter.

Condition matters — annealed vs pre-hardened

4140 and 4340 are available in annealed (~200 BHN, ~22 HRC) and pre-hardened (~28–32 HRC, sometimes to 35 HRC). Pre-hardened is what most oilfield-spec material shows up as. Both machine well; pre-hardened requires slightly more disciplined feeds and better tooling.

Post-machining heat treatment can push 4140/4340 to 40+ HRC for strength-critical parts. Coordinate with the heat-treat vendor on dimensional stock.

Common applications in oilfield

  • Wellhead spools and adapters — 4140 pre-hardened is the workhorse for API-6A adjacent wellhead components.
  • Downhole tool bodies — 4140 and 4340 pre-hardened for tool bodies requiring toughness + hardness combination.
  • Frac pump structural components — 4340 for the highest-stress components; 4140 for standard structural.
  • Shafts and mandrels — Both alloys for pump shafts, drive mandrels, and long turned components requiring straightness.
  • Reverse-engineered legacy parts — Common substitute material when original spec is unclear — good baseline for legacy oilfield hardware.

Common mistakes

  • Using annealed feeds on pre-hardened stock — Premature tool wear; poor finish. Reduce speed ~30% on pre-hardened.
  • Skipping stock allowance for post-machining heat treatment — Aging shrinks parts. Coordinate with heat-treat vendor.
  • Cheap HSS on pre-hardened stock — Doesn't survive; use coated carbide.

What tolerance can be held on 4140 and 4340 steel?

±0.0005" routine on critical features with sharp coated carbide, controlled feeds and speeds, and CMM verification. Tighter possible with the right fixture and setup discipline.

What's the difference between 4140 and 4340?

4340 has additional nickel (~1.65%) for higher toughness and better through-hardenability in larger sections. 4140 is more common and slightly cheaper. Both machine similarly.

Can 4140 be machined in fully hardened condition?

Above ~40 HRC, standard turning/milling struggles. Grinding is often the finishing operation for through-hardened parts. Pre-hardened (~28–32 HRC) is the practical machining limit for most operations.

What's the ideal insert grade for pre-hardened 4140?

TiCN-TiN coated carbide for standard work; AlTiN for high-speed or aggressive roughing. Sandvik GC4325, Kennametal KC5010, or equivalent grades.

Is 4140/4340 the same as 4130?

Related — 4130 has slightly lower carbon (0.30% vs 0.40%), used for weldable structural applications where hardenability isn't critical. Machines slightly easier than 4140.
